# Writing Process: From Idea to Submission

## Define the Nugget

Before writing a single word, answer these questions:

1. **What problem needs solving and why should anyone care?**
2. **What is the key insight** (the "nugget") that makes your problem solvable?
3. **Who is your audience?** (clinicians, methodologists, policy makers?)
4. **What is the testable hypothesis?**
5. **Can you pitch this in 3 sentences?** (problem → insight → result)
6. **How will you evaluate success** — what is the primary outcome?

The nugget is NOT your technical contribution (e.g., "we used trajectory modeling"). It is the **insight** that makes the contribution possible (e.g., "the dynamic pattern of a biomarker carries more prognostic information than any single measurement"). If you cannot articulate the nugget, you are not ready to write.

### Goal → Problem → Solution

Structure your paper like a story. This pattern repeats at every level — the whole paper, each section, each paragraph:

- **Goal**: What do we want? (predict ICU mortality earlier)
- **Problem**: What stands in the way? (current tools use single measurements, missing dynamic information)
- **Solution**: How do we resolve it? (trajectory modeling captures the dynamic pattern)

If a paragraph does not fit this arc or advance one, question whether it belongs.

## Title Strategy

Your title should condense the nugget, not just name the topic.

| Weak | Strong |
|------|--------|
| "A Study of LDH Levels in ICU Patients" | "Serial LDH Trajectories Predict 90-Day Mortality in Critically Ill Patients" |
| "Analysis of Biomarker Patterns and Outcomes" | "Dynamic Biomarker Trajectories Outperform Single Measurements in Predicting ICU Mortality" |

### Rules

- Include the **population**, **exposure/predictor**, and **outcome** — make it searchable
- No abbreviations unless universally recognized (ICU, HIV, COVID-19)
- Stay under the target journal's character limit
- Avoid "Novel" or "New" — the novelty should be obvious from the content

## Write Shitty First Drafts Early

Perfection is revision, not first-pass writing. Get words on paper.

### Suggested Writing Order

1. **Figures and tables first** — they ARE the paper's backbone. If figures alone tell the story, your argument is solid.
2. **Methods** — easiest to write; you know what you did.
3. **Results** — describe what the figures and tables show.
4. **Discussion** — interpret what Results mean.
5. **Introduction** — now you know what argument you need to set up.
6. **Abstract** — last, because it summarizes everything.

### The First Draft's Job

Its job is to **exist**, not to be good. Resist the urge to polish sentences before the argument is complete. Structure first, prose second.

## Co-Authorship Strategy

- **Never send a rough draft to your senior author.** Polish grammar, structure, and argument first.
- Senior authors should focus on **scientific argument refinement**, not copyediting.
- Result: faster turnaround, higher-quality feedback, stronger working relationship.
- When sharing, highlight specific questions: "I'm unsure whether the Discussion's biological mechanism layer is convincing — please focus there."

## Reviewer Psychology

Reviewers must: (1) summarize your paper in 3–5 sentences, (2) identify strengths, (3) evaluate significance and validity, (4) pinpoint weaknesses.

### How to Support Each Need

| Reviewer Task | What They Look For | How to Help |
|--------------|-------------------|-------------|
| Summarize | Core idea in 3 sentences | Crystal-clear abstract + Discussion opener |
| Identify strengths | What's new and important | Explicit contribution statements in Introduction's final paragraph |
| Evaluate validity | Are experiments convincing? | Sensitivity analyses with clear reporting; preempt obvious objections |
| Find weaknesses | Flaws, gaps, overclaims | Honest limitations section — they will find them regardless |

### Common Reviewer Frustrations

- Cannot quickly find the main contribution → bury your lede less
- Results are a wall of numbers without narrative → organize by research question
- Limitations feel perfunctory → use acknowledge → mitigate → contextualize
- Claims exceed the evidence → match hedge level to inference strength

If a reviewer cannot summarize your paper after one read, your structure has failed — not the reviewer.

## Proofreading Protocol

### Multi-Pass Strategy

1. **Content read**: Does the argument flow logically from Introduction through Conclusion?
2. **Detail read**: Every word, caption, abbreviation definition, table/figure reference
3. **Naïve read**: Pretend you are seeing the paper for the first time — where do you get confused?
4. **Hostile read**: Read as a reviewer looking for reasons to reject — what would you attack?
5. **Number check**: Do all numbers in text match their corresponding tables and figures?

### Non-Negotiable Checks

- [ ] At least **two co-authors** proofread independently
- [ ] **Bibliography verified** — assume it has errors (it does). Check every citation's year, authors, journal, and title
- [ ] Search the PDF for "?" (missing references or cross-references)
- [ ] All abbreviations defined at first use
- [ ] Consistent spelling throughout (especially British vs. American)

## End-Game Tactics

When the deadline approaches:

- **"Dance with the one who brung ya"** — focus on your actual contributions. A smaller, well-told story beats an incomplete ambitious narrative.
- If an analysis is not done **two weeks before submission**, seriously consider cutting it. An unfinished analysis weakens the whole paper.
- Fill page limits with substance from existing results (expanded figures, additional sensitivity analyses), not filler.
- Resist the urge to add "one more thing" — every late addition is a risk to the sections that already work.

## Checklist

- [ ] Nugget defined and articulable in one sentence
- [ ] Elevator pitch written (3 sentences: problem → insight → result)
- [ ] Title condenses the nugget with population, exposure, and outcome
- [ ] Figures drafted before prose
- [ ] Writing order followed: figures → Methods → Results → Discussion → Introduction → Abstract
- [ ] Senior author receives a polished draft, not a rough one
- [ ] Paper structured to support reviewer tasks (summarize, evaluate, critique)
- [ ] Multi-pass proofreading completed (content → detail → naïve → hostile → numbers)
- [ ] Bibliography verified for accuracy
- [ ] No last-minute unfinished analyses included
