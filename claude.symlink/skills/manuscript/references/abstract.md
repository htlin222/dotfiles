# Abstract: The Standalone Argument

The abstract is often the only part of your paper that most people read. It must function as a complete, self-contained argument — not a table of contents.

## Structured Medical Abstract

Most biomedical journals require structured abstracts with these sections:

| Section | Purpose | Length |
|---------|---------|--------|
| **Background** | Why this study matters and what gap it fills | 2–3 sentences |
| **Methods** | Study design, data source, analytical approach | 2–3 sentences |
| **Results** | Primary finding with effect size and precision | 2–4 sentences |
| **Conclusions** | Clinical or scientific implication | 1–2 sentences |

## The Goal → Problem → Solution Rhythm

Each section follows a micro-arc of the same narrative pattern:

- **Background**: Goal (what we want to achieve) → Problem (why current approaches fall short)
- **Methods**: Solution approach (how we address the gap)
- **Results**: Evidence (what we found, with numbers)
- **Conclusions**: Implication (so what — for the clinician or scientist)

The reader should feel a building momentum from "here is a problem" to "here is a credible answer."

## Mad Libs Template (Medical)

Use as a starting scaffold, then polish:

> **Background:** [Clinical condition] remains [specific challenge] because [gap in current knowledge/tools]. No study has [specific unaddressed question].
>
> **Methods:** We conducted a [study design] using [data source] including [N] [population]. [Primary analytical method] was used to [specific aim]. [Key methodological detail, e.g., adjustment strategy or validation].
>
> **Results:** [Primary finding with effect size and 95% CI]. [Secondary finding or subgroup result]. [Sensitivity analysis confirmation, if space allows].
>
> **Conclusions:** [Clinical implication — what should change or be investigated next]. [Concrete next step, not "more research is needed"].

### Example

> **Background:** Early identification of deteriorating ICU patients remains challenging because current prognostic tools rely on single-timepoint measurements, missing the dynamic trajectory of disease. No study has examined whether serial biomarker patterns independently predict short-term mortality.
>
> **Methods:** We conducted a retrospective cohort study of 2,847 ICU admissions from [database]. Group-based trajectory modeling identified distinct LDH trajectory patterns over the first 7 days. Cox regression assessed the association between trajectory group and 90-day mortality, adjusting for age, severity, and comorbidities.
>
> **Results:** Three LDH trajectories emerged: stable-low (41%), transient-rise (35%), and persistent-climb (24%). The persistent-climb trajectory was independently associated with 2.4-fold higher 90-day mortality (HR 2.4, 95% CI 1.8–3.2) compared to stable-low. Results were consistent across sensitivity analyses.
>
> **Conclusions:** Serial LDH trajectory monitoring may provide a dynamic prognostic tool in ICU settings. Prospective validation should evaluate whether trajectory-guided decisions improve patient outcomes.

## Common Abstract Mistakes

| Mistake | Problem | Fix |
|---------|---------|-----|
| Background too broad | "Cancer is a leading cause of death" tells the reader nothing | State the specific gap your study addresses |
| Results without effect sizes | "A significant association was found" | Report HR/OR/RR with 95% CI |
| Conclusions exceed the data | "This will change clinical practice" | State what the findings *suggest*, with appropriate hedging |
| Missing the "so what" | Conclusions restate the finding | State the clinical implication or concrete next step |
| Vague methods | "Statistical analyses were performed" | Name the specific design, method, and outcome |
| Claims absent from main text | Abstract promises something Discussion doesn't deliver | Write the abstract last; verify every claim is supported |

## Checklist

- [ ] Follows journal's required structured format
- [ ] Background states the **specific gap**, not a textbook opening
- [ ] Methods names the design, data source, sample size, and primary method
- [ ] Results reports the primary outcome with **effect size and 95% CI**
- [ ] Conclusions states a **clinical implication**, not just the statistical finding
- [ ] No claims absent from the main text
- [ ] Goal → Problem → Solution rhythm is evident across sections
- [ ] Word count within journal limit
